The Heterogeneity of Profitability Among U.S. Listed Firms, 2015-2025

Developed for the MBA course Economics of Strategic Behavior at Columbia Business School.

Why Do Some Firms Win So Much More Than Others?

Which firms are persistently more economically successful than others? The heterogeneity is large. But only a small fraction of this dispersion is explained by the industry in which a firm competes: industry membership explains about 12.4% of Operating ROA variation and about 4.0% of Operating Margin variation. Rather than where to compete, the key question we should ask is thus how to compete.

The All Firms tab shows the unconditional distribution of profitability across firms, using each firm's average value across available fiscal years. The By Industry tab breaks the same firm averages into industry groups, reports each industry's median, and shows how much of the total variance is explained by differences across industries rather than differences among firms inside the same industry.

There are two important caveats. First, profitability is hard to measure perfectly from public data at this scale. Ideally we would measure economic profit, such as ROIC minus cost of capital, but that is difficult to compute cleanly for hundreds of firms from raw public filings. I therefore use two practical, imperfect measures: Operating ROA, which asks how much operating profit a firm generates per dollar of assets, and Operating Margin, which asks how much operating profit the firm keeps from each dollar of sales. ROA is useful because it adjusts for asset intensity; margin is useful because it is intuitive and easy to interpret.

Second, industry definitions are imperfect. Here, firms are classified into 20 broad industries using the company descriptions we collected, primarily Wikipedia extracts, with manual rules and residual assignment when a firm does not fit cleanly. This is still a simplification, but it is more granular and economically meaningful than relying only on stock-market sector labels. The striking result is that even this industry classification explains only a small share of profitability differences.

Variance Decomposition

This decomposes the cross-firm variance of the selected metric into two pieces. I first use one average value per firm and drop firms assigned to Residual / other. The between-industry share compares each industry's mean profitability with the overall mean, weighted by the number of firms in that industry. The within-industry share captures the remaining dispersion of firms around their own industry mean. These two shares sum to 100%; the between-industry share is equivalent to the R-squared from a regression of the selected metric on industry fixed effects.

Data Notes

We start from 1,000 firms in the iShares Russell 1000 ETF holdings file, as of May 27, 2026; 992 have a non-empty initial industry label. We match firms to SEC Company Facts, collapse duplicate share classes to one economic entity, and drop firms with fewer than 5 usable fiscal years for either Operating ROA or Operating Margin. In this run, 1 ticker did not match a SEC CIK, 9 duplicate share-class/security rows were removed, and 104 firms failed the 5-year ratio-history rule. The final graphing panel has 861 firms and 8,444 firm-year observations. Each dot is one firm's average metric across its available fiscal years; averages above 100% or below -100% are hidden as outliers. Firms are grouped using one primary industry from a fixed set of 20 broad industries plus Residual / other. Industries are assigned from the firm descriptions we collected, primarily Wikipedia extracts with cached company-description fallbacks; secondary industries are kept for context but are not used to place dots in the industry view. Current industry totals: 515 non-residual firms and 346 residual firms.
