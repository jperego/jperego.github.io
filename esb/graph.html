The Heterogeneity of Profitability Among U.S. Listed Firms, 2015-2025

Developed by Jacopo Perego for the MBA course Economics of Strategic Behavior at Columbia Business School.

Introduction

Why are some firms persistently more successful than others?

This is one of the central questions in strategy and industrial organization. Some companies generate high profits year after year, while others struggle, even when they appear to operate in similar markets and face similar customers, suppliers, technologies, and competitors.

The data in this page show that profitability varies enormously across U.S. listed companies. But surprisingly, only a modest share of this variation is explained by the industry in which a firm competes. In this sample, industry membership explains about 18.1% of the variation in Operating ROA and only about 4.3% of the variation in Operating Margin.

This creates an important puzzle. If firms in the same industry face broadly similar economic conditions, why do they often perform so differently? Why are some firms able to earn much higher returns than their peers? One of the key goals of this course is to understand that puzzle.

This page documents this empirical fact and allows you to explore it directly, hands-on. The All Firms tab shows the overall distribution of profitability across companies, using each firm's average profitability across the fiscal years available in the data. The By Industry tab breaks these same firm-level averages into selected industry groups. It reports each industry's median profitability and shows how much of the total variation comes from differences across industries versus differences among firms within the same industry.

There are two important caveats to keep in mind.

First, profitability is difficult to measure perfectly using public data at this scale. Ideally, we would like to measure economic profit: for example, whether a firm earns returns above its cost of capital. But that is difficult to compute cleanly for hundreds of companies using raw public filings. We therefore use two practical, imperfect measures. Operating ROA asks how much operating profit a firm generates per dollar of assets. Operating Margin asks how much operating profit the firm keeps from each dollar of sales. ROA is useful because it accounts for how asset-intensive a business is. Margin is useful because it is intuitive and easy to interpret.

Second, industry definitions are imperfect. Here, firms are classified with LLM into a fixed set of broad industries using detailed company descriptions extracted from Wikipedia. Firms that do not fit cleanly into one of the predefined categories are assigned to a residual category. This classification is still a simplification, but it is more granular and economically meaningful than relying only on broad stock-market sector labels.

The striking result is that even this more detailed industry classification explains only a limited share of profitability differences. A great deal of the action is not simply across industries, but within them. That is where strategy becomes especially important: understanding why some firms, facing similar broad industry conditions, build stronger positions, make better choices, and achieve persistently superior performance.

Variance Decomposition

This decomposes the cross-firm variance of the selected metric into two pieces. I first use one average value per firm and drop firms assigned to Residual / other. The between-industry share compares each industry's mean profitability with the overall mean, weighted by the number of firms in that industry. The within-industry share captures the remaining dispersion of firms around their own industry mean. These two shares sum to 100%; the between-industry share is equivalent to the R-squared from a regression of the selected metric on industry fixed effects.

Data Notes

We start from 1,000 firms in the iShares Russell 1000 ETF holdings file, as of May 27, 2026; 992 have a non-empty initial industry label. We match firms to SEC Company Facts, collapse duplicate share classes to one economic entity, and keep firms with at least 5 usable fiscal years for Operating ROA or Operating Margin. In this run, 1 ticker did not match a SEC CIK, 9 duplicate share-class/security rows were removed, and 47 firms had fewer than 5 usable years for both metrics. The final graphing panel has 918 firms and 8,874 firm-year observations. Each dot is one firm's average for the selected metric, shown only when the firm has all 10 fiscal years for that metric; averages above 100% or below -100% are hidden as outliers. Firms are grouped using one primary industry from a fixed set of broad industries plus Residual / other. Industries are assigned from the firm descriptions we collected, primarily Wikipedia extracts with cached company-description fallbacks; secondary industries are kept for context but are not used to place dots in the industry view. Current industry totals: 634 non-residual firms and 284 residual firms.
